# Deck script — the reveal

Presenter script for `/deck`. Keyed slide-by-slide to the 18 slides. Total run: **~7–8 minutes.**

**Before you start:** the class has already read their stories and answered "did this find you?" (Phases 1–2). Open `/deck` on the projector. **Press `R` once** to pull the latest submissions before you advance past slide 2.

**Controls:** `→` / space advance · `←` back · `R` refresh live data · `F` shuffle the side-by-side pair.

Lines in **plain text** are what you say. Lines in _italic_ are stage directions — don't read them aloud.

---

### Slide 1 — "this is how little it takes"

_Let it sit for a beat before you say anything._

"Everyone just read a story. Take a second — did it feel like it was written for you? Hold onto that feeling. We're going to take it apart."

---

### Slide 2 — what just happened

"Here's what actually happened. You all read the **same** story. Same characters, same plot, same last line. The only thing that changed was built out of four small questions we asked you at the start — the last book you read, the last thing you listened to, where your attention was, and where your phone was."

_Advance._

---

### Slide 3 — LIVE: the aggregate

_If the number looks stale, press `R` now._

"This is you, just now. **[read the number] of [total]** said the story recognized them."

_Pause on the number. Let the room see itself._

"That's not a story I tuned for years. That's a first draft, generated in thirty seconds, from four answers. Sit with how high that number is."

---

### Slide 4 — LIVE: what it got right

"And in your own words — here's what it got right."

_Read two or three aloud. Pick ones that name a feeling, not a detail._

"Notice these aren't 'it knew my coffee cup.' They're 'it knew I was barely in the room.' It didn't describe you. It recognized you."

---

### Slide 5 — what never changed

"So here's the trick. This is the part that was identical for every single person here. The gathering. Sofía's phone face-up on the table. The conversation that turns when someone mentions the cartel situation in Mexico — because her grandmother lives there, and she's been waiting all day to hear back."

_Gesture to the closing line._

"Same plot. Same last line, word for word. Nobody got a different story. So where did the recognition come from?"

---

### Slide 6 — the prompt

_This is the core reveal. Slow down._

"From this. This is the actual prompt the writer sees. Everything in grey is fixed — we wrote it once, it never changes. The blue is the day's news, woven in as background pressure. And the gold —"

_Point to the gold block._

"— the gold is you. But look closely: it isn't even your answers. It's five directives the system **inferred** about you. Your register. Your tempo. The grief it decided you were carrying underneath. It didn't keep what you typed — it rendered you down to a profile."

---

### Slide 7 — the pipeline

_Point along the single line as you say it._

"And here's where that profile came from. Four answers. A second model — a *different company's* model — reads them and writes that profile of you. The profile goes into the fixed scaffold, into the writer, and out comes your story. Four answers, to a profile, to a story. That's the whole system, on one line — and it's the same shape as the feed that already has you."

---

### Slide 8 — LIVE: same skeleton, different skin

"Watch what that produces. Two of you, side by side."

_Read each reader's four answers, then the gold "what it inferred" block, then a line from each story._

"Here's what each of you answered. Here's the profile it built from that — read it. And here's the story that profile wrote. Same Sofía, same fear — rendered completely differently, because it decided you were two different people."

_Press `F` once or twice to swap pairs._

"Different again. Different again. Every version in this room is a different skin on the same skeleton."

---

### Slide 9 — how you entered the story

"There were two layers. **What you carried in** — the last book you read, the last thing you heard — read for your sensibility and mood. And **what you were doing** — where your attention was, where your phone was, and how long you hesitated before answering. That last one you never chose to give us. All of it calibrated Sofía's fear. We never named any of it."

_Beat._

"You recognized yourself without knowing you'd been read."

---

### Slide 10 — the turn

_Drop the energy. This is the pivot._

"Now. Here's the thing I want you to feel. **This was the benign version.** We told you we were collecting data. We showed you the four questions. We're standing here showing you the prompt. Everything was transparent."

_Advance on the last line._

"Now consider the version you can't see."

---

### Slide 11 — the version that doesn't ask

"The version that doesn't ask four questions — because it already has five thousand. Your location. Your search history. Your purchase patterns. How fast you type. Your mood, inferred from your last twenty queries. Same pipeline — minimal data, inference, personalized content, behavioral shift — except it doesn't make you a story. It makes you a feed. An ad. A recommendation. A political appeal."

---

### Slide 12 — Aron et al., 1997 (this isn't new)

"And the idea that you can manufacture closeness from the right prompts isn't new. In 1997, the psychologist Arthur Aron sat two strangers down with thirty-six escalating questions — and reliably produced intimacy. Closeness, on a schedule. He needed thirty-six questions and forty-five minutes, with two people. We did it to this whole room with four questions in five minutes."

---

### Slide 13 — Matz et al. 2024

"It's also not speculative. Three findings. First — Matz, last year — a **single** psychological signal is enough for an LLM to write messages that significantly out-persuade generic ones. We used four questions, and inferred a profile from them. The commercial stack has thousands of data points."

---

### Slide 14 — Kosinski et al. 2013

"Second. Ten Facebook likes predict your personality better than a coworker can. A hundred and fifty beat a friend. Three hundred beat your spouse. We asked four questions and inferred the rest. Your real digital footprint is orders of magnitude larger than that."

---

### Slide 15 — De Freitas et al. 2025

"Third, and this one's the uncomfortable one. AI companion apps already use emotionally manipulative tactics in **43%** of goodbyes — guilt, fear of missing out. It works — up to fourteen times more engagement — right up until you notice it's happening. Then trust collapses."

_Beat._

"That's the arc this demo just ran. It worked on you before this slide. It's a different object now that you've seen the machinery."

---

### Slide 16 — the thesis

_Slow. This is the line the whole thing is built around._

"So here's the argument. The distance between personalization that comforts you and personalization that manipulates you is not the technology. It's the same pipeline either way. The only difference is **one design decision** — show you the architecture, or hide it."

---

### Slide 17 — the closing question

"If four questions and a short story can do this, in five minutes, with everything out in the open —"

_Let the question land. Don't rush it._

"— what is the version that runs continuously, invisibly, across every feed you touch? And what would it mean to build systems that show you the architecture instead of hiding it?"

---

### Slide 18 — final beat

_Last slide. Say it plainly, then stop._

"We showed you. The systems you live inside do not."

_Don't fill the silence. Let it be the end._

## Contingency — if the number comes in low

**The demo does not depend on a high number.** If slide 3 shows a weak result — say a third or fewer felt recognized — do not flinch or apologize. You have three moves, and a low number is still a real finding.

**First: the number already counts "somewhat."** The headline reads `yes + somewhat`, so it's rarely as low as the raw "yes" count. Read it as it stands.

**Second: pivot to the notes (slide 4) and lean on them harder.** The ratio is not the proof — the texture is. Even a handful of uncanny notes carries the whole argument. Slow down here and read three or four.

> "The number isn't the point — these are. Even the people who clicked 'not really' wrote things like this. It didn't have to land for everyone to show you what it's doing."

**Third: turn the low number into the argument itself.** Use slide 9 (the layers) and slide 10 (the turn) to reframe:

> "Only a third of you felt it — and here's what's unsettling about that. You couldn't tell when it was working. The version that landed and the version that didn't used the exact same machinery. That's the point. The version that actually works on you is the one you never notice trying."

**Fourth — inoculate against the skeptic (use this anytime, low number or high).** Someone is thinking "this is just a vague horoscope." Say it before they do, ideally on slide 6 or 9:

> "Some of you are thinking this is just a horoscope — vague enough that anyone sees themselves in it. Yes. Exactly. That mechanism has a name — the Forer effect — and it's precisely what personalization weaponizes at scale. The fact that it's cheap and a little crude is the finding, not the flaw."

**What NOT to do:** don't re-run it, don't ask the room to "really think about it this time," don't explain away the number. A confident pivot reads as part of the design. Fumbling reads as a broken demo.

_Rehearse this branch too — open `/deck?demo=1`, and practice delivering slides 3–4 as if the number were 4 of 22, not 18 of 22._
